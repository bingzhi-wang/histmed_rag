Hippocrates’ *On Ancient Medicine* (De prisca medicina) argues that the origins of the medical art stem from the recognition that sick individuals require different dietary regimens than healthy ones. The text asserts that medicine emerged out of necessity, as sick people did not benefit from the same "mode of living and regimen" as healthy individuals, unlike animals such as oxen or horses, which thrive on unprocessed natural foods like fruits, grass, and wood [SOURCE 2]. This divergence in dietary needs between health and disease necessitated the development of medical practices to address imbalances, with early practitioners experimenting to adapt foods to human constitutions by modifying their strength and quantity [SOURCE 3].  

Regarding diet’s role, the text emphasizes that proper nourishment is central to medical care. It contrasts the unprocessed diets of animals with human dietary practices, noting that early humans likely consumed raw, unrefined foods but later developed methods like baking and boiling to make strong, uncompounded foods more digestible. This adaptation aimed to prevent pain, disease, and death by aligning food with human physiology, as foods too strong for the body to assimilate were deemed harmful, while those that could be assimilated promoted health [SOURCE 2]. The text also highlights the importance of adjusting food quantities and qualities for patients, such as diluting strong foods with water to create weaker nourishment for those unable to tolerate regular diets [SOURCE 3].  

The passages do not explicitly discuss the origins of medicine beyond dietary practices or address non-dietary aspects of medical art. They also do not compare medical traditions outside the Greek context, as the retrieved sources for Chinese (*Huangdi Neijing Suwen*) and medieval European (*Causae et Curae*) medicine are not relevant to the specific arguments in *On Ancient Medicine*.
