The three medical traditions—ancient Chinese, Greek, and medieval European—differ significantly in their explanations of how seasonal changes relate to disease onset, as evidenced by the provided sources.  

### **Ancient Chinese Medicine**  
The *Huangdi Neijing Suwen* explicitly links seasonal shifts to specific disease patterns through the concept of *qi* (氣) cycles. For example:  
- **Cold and heat alternations** in specific seasons are directly tied to disease manifestations. The "fourth qi" associates cold rain with sudden collapse, shivering, delirium, and conditions like "bone atrophy" and "blood diarrhea" [SOURCE 1]. Similarly, the "fourth qi" in another passage connects humid heat and frequent rain to alternating cold/heat symptoms, jaundice, and nasal discharge [SOURCE 5].  
- **Seasonal dietary and therapeutic adjustments** are emphasized to harmonize the body’s *qi*. For instance, during the "final qi" (associated with warmth), recommendations include consuming "annual grain" to stabilize *qi* and using bitter or sour flavors to address imbalances [SOURCE 1]. Spring’s "command reversal" is linked to harmonious *qi* and specific illnesses like "warmth" (溫), requiring tailored interventions [SOURCE 1].  
- **Yin-yang and seasonal rhythms** are central to understanding disease. The text warns that failing to align treatments with seasonal *qi* (e.g., using "heat far from heat") disrupts "heavenly-earth principles" and "yin-yang records" [SOURCE 1].  

### **Greek Medicine**  
Hippocratic texts focus on **humoral imbalances** and environmental factors but do not explicitly tie seasonal changes to disease onset in the same cyclical, structured way as Chinese medicine. Instead:  
- **Seasonal prognosis** is discussed in *Prognosticon* (Section 25), noting that the "constitution of the season" and "epidemical diseases" influence disease outcomes. However, this addresses **prognosis**, not causation [SOURCE 11].  
- **Humoral theory** (four humors: blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile) underpins disease, but seasonal shifts are not directly linked to humoral imbalances. For example, *De Prisca Medicina* (Section 19) describes how purging bitter humors (yellow bile) alleviates fever, but this is framed as a general therapeutic principle, not a seasonal one [SOURCE 7].  
- **Dietary adjustments** are recommended for individual patients, but these are not explicitly tied to seasonal cycles [SOURCE 12].  

### **Medieval European Medicine (Hildegard von Bingen)**  
The *Causae et Curae* emphasizes **humoral balance** and divine intervention but lacks explicit seasonal frameworks for disease causation. Key points include:  
- **Humoral expulsion** through sweat is described as a process influenced by divine grace, but no seasonal timing is specified [SOURCE 9].  
- **Dietary and environmental factors** (e.g., "humidity of foods") affect humors, but again, no direct connection to seasonal cycles is made [SOURCE 10].  
- **Emotional states** (e.g., fear, joy) are linked to disease, but these are not tied to seasonal changes [SOURCE 9].  

### **Comparative Synthesis**  
- **Chinese medicine** provides the most detailed and systematic linkage between seasonal *qi* cycles and disease, with specific pathological syndromes and therapeutic strategies tied to each season [SOURCE 1, 5].  
- **Greek and medieval European traditions** focus on humoral imbalances and environmental factors but do not explicitly frame seasonal changes as causal agents for disease onset. Instead, they address **prognosis** or **general therapeutic principles** [SOURCE 7, 11, 9].  
- **Gaps**: The Greek and medieval European sources do not contain explicit discussions of seasonal *qi* or cyclical disease patterns tied to the calendar year, unlike the Chinese texts [SOURCE 1, 5].  

In summary, ancient Chinese medicine uniquely integrates seasonal *qi* dynamics into its etiology and treatment, while Greek and medieval European traditions emphasize humoral balance and environmental influences without a structured seasonal framework for disease causation.
