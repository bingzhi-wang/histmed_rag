The Greek humoral theory and Chinese yin-yang theory both conceptualize disease as arising from imbalances in the body’s internal dynamics, but they differ in their frameworks, mechanisms, and therapeutic approaches. Below is a comparative synthesis grounded in the provided passages:

---

### **1. Conceptual Foundations**  
- **Greek Humoral Theory**: Health depends on the balance of four humors—blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile—each associated with specific qualities (hot, cold, dry, moist). Disease arises when these humors are in excess or deficiency, disrupting the body’s equilibrium. For example, an overabundance of yellow bile (a "hot" and "dry" humor) causes symptoms like nausea, burning sensations, and fever, requiring counteraction with opposites (e.g., cold or moist treatments) to restore balance [SOURCE 10].  
- **Chinese Yin-Yang Theory**: Health is maintained through the dynamic equilibrium of yin (cold, passive, moist) and yang (hot, active, dry). Imbalance occurs when one force dominates, leading to disease. For instance, excessive "heat" (yang excess) might manifest as upper-body symptoms like throat pain or fever, while "cold" (yin excess) could cause lower-body issues like abdominal pain or cold extremities [SOURCE 4]. The interplay of yin and yang is further mediated by the Five Elements (wood, fire, earth, metal, water), which interact through principles of generation and overcoming [SOURCE 8].

---

### **2. Mechanisms of Disease**  
- **Greek Theory**: Disease is attributed to the "overpowering" of one humor over another. For example, if pathogenic influences (e.g., excessive yellow bile) reach a part of the body they cannot "overcome," symptoms worsen; if they reach a part they can overcome, symptoms improve [SOURCE 4]. The body’s response is tied to the intrinsic qualities of the humors and their interactions with external factors.  
- **Chinese Theory**: Disease arises from the disruption of qi (vital energy) and the imbalance of yin-yang. Pathogenic qi (e.g., "cold" or "heat") invades the body, interacting with the body’s own qi according to principles of overcoming. For example, "cold" pathogenic qi might cause "yang deficiency," leading to symptoms like cold extremities and fatigue, while "heat" pathogenic qi might cause "yin deficiency," resulting in fever and dryness [SOURCE 4]. The Five Elements further structure these interactions, as seen in the association of specific organs (e.g., liver with wood) and their susceptibility to imbalances [SOURCE 8].

---

### **3. Therapeutic Approaches**  
- **Greek Theory**: Treatment involves using opposites to counteract imbalances. For example, cold is used to treat heat, and moist to treat dryness. This is evident in recommendations to consume foods or apply therapies that directly oppose the excess humor (e.g., bitter foods to counteract excessive yellow bile) [SOURCE 10].  
- **Chinese Theory**: Therapies aim to restore harmony between yin and yang, often through dietary adjustments, herbal medicine, and acupuncture. For instance, the text emphasizes using specific tastes (e.g., salty, bitter, sour) to regulate the body’s qi and balance the Five Elements [SOURCE 6]. Treatments may also involve addressing the root cause of imbalance, such as using acupuncture points or bloodletting to resolve stagnation or excess [SOURCE 4].

---

### **4. Key Differences**  
- **Framework**: Greek theory is rooted in the four humors and their qualitative properties (hot, cold, dry, moist), while Chinese theory is based on the dynamic interplay of yin and yang, mediated by the Five Elements.  
- **Pathogenesis**: Greek theory focuses on the intrinsic qualities of humors and their direct opposition, whereas Chinese theory emphasizes the flow of qi and the complex interactions between yin-yang and the Five Elements.  
- **Treatment**: Greek approaches prioritize direct opposition (e.g., hot for cold), while Chinese methods often seek to harmonize rather than oppose, using principles like the Five Elements to guide interventions [SOURCE 6].

---

### **5. Overlaps and Similarities**  
Both systems recognize that health depends on balance and that disease results from its disruption. They also link specific symptoms to particular imbalances (e.g., heat-related symptoms in Greek theory vs. yin deficiency in Chinese theory). Additionally, both traditions emphasize the role of diet and lifestyle in maintaining equilibrium [SOURCE 4, SOURCE 6].

---

### **Limitations of the Sources**  
The passages do not explicitly compare the two systems directly. Greek sources focus narrowly on humoral qualities and their opposites, while Chinese texts elaborate on yin-yang and the Five Elements but do not engage with humoral theory. Thus, the comparison is inferred from their respective frameworks, without direct dialogue between the traditions.
