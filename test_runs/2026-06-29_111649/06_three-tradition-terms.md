The vital principles in the Chinese, Greek, and Hildegardian medical traditions are distinct in their conceptual frameworks, as evidenced by the retrieved passages. Below is a comparative synthesis grounded strictly in the provided texts:

---

### **Chinese Medicine: Vital Qi (shēng qì) and Seasonal Harmony**  
In Chinese medicine, **vital qi (shēng qì)** is central to health, described as the "fundamental root (gēn) of all things" and the basis of life and death. The *Huangdi Neijing Suwen* emphasizes that violating the seasonal rhythms (e.g., spring qi, summer qi) disrupts the balance of internal organs and their corresponding qi, leading to illness. For example, violating spring qi causes "Shaoyang [meridian] not to arise" and "liver qi [gān qì] internally transforms" [SOURCE 1]. The text prescribes harmonizing with the seasons by "nourishing yang in spring and summer, and yin in autumn and winter" to sustain vital qi and prevent disease. This principle underscores a dynamic interplay between the body’s qi and external environmental forces, mediated through yin-yang and the four seasons.

---

### **Greek Medicine: Humoral Balance and Critique of Reductionism**  
The Greek passages from *On Ancient Medicine* focus on the **four humors (heat, cold, dryness, moisture)** as foundational to health, though they critique simplistic postulates that reduce disease to a single factor. Hippocrates argues that medical practice requires understanding the complex interplay of these humors rather than relying on isolated principles like "heat" or "cold" [SOURCE 10]. For instance, he critiques those who claim that "cold" or "heat" alone causes disease, emphasizing that effective treatment requires adjusting diet and lifestyle to restore balance (e.g., replacing raw meat and water with cooked food and wine for a patient with a weakened constitution) [SOURCE 12]. However, the texts do not explicitly mention **innate heat** or **physis (φύσις)** as defined terms. While "physis" might be inferred as the body’s natural state, the passages prioritize the humoral theory over explicit references to innate qualities.

---

### **Hildegard of Bingen: Viriditas, Humoral Vitality, and Elemental Interplay**  
Hildegard’s *Causae et Curae* integrates **viriditas** (greenness, vitality) and humoral balance, framed through elemental and spiritual metaphors. **Viriditas** is described as the "work of the word" sustained by "fire and heat," with water serving as a medium that "vivifies and mortifies" [SOURCE 2]. Water is also linked to "humor" (one of its fifteen powers) and is essential for bodily functions, such as "retaining reptiles in its foam" and "making beasts live by its heat" [SOURCE 3]. The soul is equated with **fire**, which "penetrates the entire body and vivifies man" [SOURCE 8], suggesting a vital connection between spiritual fire and physical vitality. Humoral vitality is further tied to the interplay of **heat, air, and humor**, with imbalances (e.g., coagulated humors) disrupting the soul’s "vital motions" [SOURCE 7]. Unlike the Chinese emphasis on seasonal harmony, Hildegard’s framework emphasizes elemental and spiritual forces, with **viriditas** as a distinct principle of life and growth.

---

### **Comparative Summary**  
- **Chinese Medicine** centers on **shēng qì** (vital qi), harmonized with seasonal and yin-yang cycles to prevent disease [SOURCE 1].  
- **Greek Medicine** prioritizes the **balance of humors** (heat, cold, dryness, moisture), though the texts do not explicitly reference "innate heat" or "physis" [SOURCE 10–12].  
- **Hildegard’s Tradition** integrates **viriditas** (a spiritual-vital principle) with humoral and elemental dynamics, emphasizing fire, water, and the soul’s role in vitality [SOURCE 2, 3, 8].  

Each tradition conceptualizes vitality through distinct metaphors—qi, humors, and viriditas—reflecting their unique cultural and philosophical contexts. The Greek texts lack explicit terms like "innate heat" or "physis," while Hildegard’s work introduces **viriditas** as a uniquely medieval European concept.
