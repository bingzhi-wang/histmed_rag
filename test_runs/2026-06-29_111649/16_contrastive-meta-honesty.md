The three medical traditions fundamentally disagree on the **primary causal mechanisms of disease**, with **evidence in the corpus being insufficient to judge** certain aspects of their comparative frameworks. Below is a structured analysis:

---

### **Fundamental Disagreements**  
1. **Chinese Medicine (Huangdi Neijing Suwen):**  
   - **Qi (氣) and Environmental Balance:** Disease arises from imbalances in **qi**, yin-yang dynamics, and interactions between the body and external forces (e.g., seasonal changes, climate, and emotional states). For example, "fourth qi" (四之氣) is linked to cold rain causing sudden collapse, shivering, and specific diseases like malaria and bone atrophy [SOURCE 1]. Similarly, "fifth qi" (五之氣) emphasizes harmonizing with environmental rhythms to prevent disease [SOURCE 3].  
   - **Internal and External Factors:** Internal causes include emotional disturbances (e.g., "loss of status and power" leading to internal injury of essence and spirit) and lifestyle changes (e.g., "from wealth to poverty" causing physical decay) [SOURCE 2].  

2. **Greek Medicine (Hippocrates, *On Ancient Medicine*):**  
   - **Humoral Imbalance:** Disease stems from **disruptions in the four humors** (blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile) and their interactions with bodily structure. For instance, structural differences (e.g., head size, neck thickness) are said to influence disease susceptibility [SOURCE 9]. However, the provided texts do **not explicitly name the four humors** but critique simplistic postulates (e.g., attributing all disease to heat or cold) [SOURCE 6].  
   - **Individual Variability:** Emphasis is placed on understanding individual anatomical and physiological differences to tailor treatment, contrasting with the Chinese focus on universal qi dynamics [SOURCE 9].  

3. **Medieval European Medicine (Hildegard von Bingen):**  
   - **Humoral Theory:** Disease is directly tied to **imbalances in humors** (e.g., "harmful humors" causing fevers when the soul’s "vital motions" are diminished) [SOURCE 11]. Treatments aim to remove "bad humors" (e.g., using fennel and butter to draw out pain from virile parts) [SOURCE 12].  
   - **Alignment with Greek Tradition:** This mirrors Greek humoral theory but adds practical applications, unlike the Greek texts, which focus on theoretical principles.  

**Key Disagreement:**  
- **Chinese vs. Greek/Medieval European:** The Chinese tradition emphasizes **qi, yin-yang, and environmental harmony**, while the Greek and medieval traditions prioritize **humoral balance** and structural individuality. For example, Chinese texts link seasonal "qi" phases to specific diseases (e.g., "fourth qi" causing malaria) [SOURCE 1], whereas Greek texts discuss structural differences (e.g., head size) as determinants of disease [SOURCE 9].  

---

### **Insufficient Evidence to Judge**  
1. **Greek Medicine’s Explicit Humoral Framework:**  
   - The provided Greek passages (e.g., *On Ancient Medicine*) **do not explicitly name the four humors** or detail their roles in disease causation. While they critique simplistic postulates [SOURCE 6], they lack the direct terminology (e.g., "humors") found in medieval European texts [SOURCE 8]. This makes it **unclear whether the Greek tradition fully aligns with the later humoral model** or diverges in its formulation.  

2. **Chinese Medicine’s Interaction with External Pathogens:**  
   - While Chinese texts mention external pathogens (e.g., "cold rain" causing disease) [SOURCE 1], they do **not explicitly compare these to the Greek concept of "external influences"** (e.g., structural differences or humoral disruptions). Thus, it is **uncertain whether Chinese and Greek traditions share a common framework** for external environmental factors.  

3. **Medieval European Medicine’s Originality:**  
   - The medieval texts (e.g., Hildegard) **do not explicitly reference Greek or Chinese traditions**, making it **impossible to judge** whether their humoral theory was influenced by Greek medicine or developed independently.  

---

### **Conclusion**  
- **Fundamental Disagreements:** The Chinese tradition attributes disease to **qi imbalances and environmental harmony**, while the Greek and medieval traditions focus on **humoral imbalances and structural individuality**.  
- **Insufficient Evidence:** The Greek texts lack explicit mention of the four humors, the Chinese texts do not compare external pathogens to Greek concepts, and the medieval texts do not reference prior traditions, leaving gaps in comparative analysis.  

This synthesis relies solely on the provided passages and avoids speculation beyond their content.
