According to the *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, each season corresponds to a specific organ and a specific aspect of *qi* (vital energy), and violating the seasonal *qi* leads to specific pathological transformations:  

1. **Spring** corresponds to the **liver** (via *Shaoyang*). Violating spring *qi* causes the liver *qi* to "internally transform" (*gān qì nèi biàn*), disrupting the liver's function [SOURCE 1].  
2. **Summer** corresponds to the **heart** (via *Taiyang*). Violating summer *qi* causes the heart *qi* to "internally hollow" (*xīn qì nèi dòng*), weakening cardiac function [SOURCE 1].  
3. **Autumn** corresponds to the **lungs** (via *Taiyin*). Violating autumn *qi* causes the lung *qi* to become "parched and full" (*fèi qì chéng pì zhì*), leading to dryness and stagnation in the lungs [SOURCE 1].  
4. **Winter** corresponds to the **kidneys** (via *Shaoyin*). Violating winter *qi* causes the kidney *qi* to "sink" (*shèn qì dú chén*), resulting in depletion of foundational *qi* and weakness in the kidneys [SOURCE 1].  

The text emphasizes that the four seasons and *yin-yang* are the "fundamental root" (*gēn*) of all life, and violating their natural rhythms ("cutting the root") leads to disease, disorder, and even death. Proper alignment with seasonal *qi*—nourishing *yang* in spring and summer, *yin* in autumn and winter—is presented as a preventive principle to avoid illness [SOURCE 1].  

No other passage in the provided texts explicitly addresses the season-organ correspondence or the consequences of violating seasonal *qi*.
