The provided passages reveal distinct approaches to understanding blood circulation across the Chinese, Greek, and medieval European medical traditions, though none explicitly describe a systemic circulatory model akin to modern physiology. Below is a comparative synthesis based on the sources:

### **Chinese Medicine (Huangdi Neijing Suwen)**  
The *Suwen* describes a dynamic interplay of *qi* (氣) and blood within the body’s meridians, emphasizing their distribution and functional roles. For instance, it states that "food qi enters the stomach, disperses essence to the liver, and excessive qi reaches the tendons" [SOURCE 7], suggesting a directional flow of *qi* and nutrients. Blood is mentioned in relation to the meridians, such as the *Taiyang* (太陽) meridian "commonly having much blood and little qi," while the *Shaoyang* (少陽) has "little blood and much qi" [SOURCE 9]. This indicates a conceptualization of blood as a substance distributed through meridians, with specific meridians associated with varying proportions of blood and *qi*. However, the text does not describe a closed-loop circulation or mechanisms like the heart driving blood flow. Instead, it focuses on the balance and interaction of *qi*, blood, and the five zang (臟) organs, with circulation tied to the meridian system’s functional roles.

### **Greek Medicine (Hippocrates)**  
The passages from *On Ancient Medicine* emphasize the balance of the four humors (blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile) as central to health [SOURCE 11]. While blood is acknowledged as one of the humors, the text does not describe its physical movement through the body. Instead, it discusses how imbalances in humors cause disease, with treatments aimed at restoring equilibrium through diet, purgation, or other interventions. For example, excessive "bitter principle" (yellow bile) is linked to nausea and fever, but the text does not explain how blood circulates or interacts with other bodily systems. The focus is on humoral theory as a framework for pathology, not on circulatory mechanics.

### **Medieval European Medicine (Hildegard von Bingen)**  
Hildegard’s *Causae et Curae* similarly centers on the four humors, describing their proportional relationships and effects on health [SOURCE 1]. Blood is mentioned in contexts of disease, such as when "bad humors" cause blood to "pour out beyond measure into the larger veins" [SOURCE 3], but this is framed as a pathological disruption rather than a normal physiological process. The text does not elaborate on a systematic circulation of blood; instead, it treats blood as one of the humors whose excess or deficiency leads to illness. For example, imbalanced humors are said to cause blood to "diffuse throughout the entire body and emitted to the intestines" [SOURCE 3], but this is described as an abnormal state, not a routine circulatory function.

### **Key Differences and Similarities**  
- **Chinese Medicine**: Integrates blood and *qi* into a meridian system, with circulation tied to the functional roles of meridians and organs. However, it lacks a mechanistic model of blood flow.  
- **Greek and Medieval European Traditions**: Both prioritize humoral balance over circulatory mechanics. Blood is treated as a humor whose excess or deficiency causes disease, but neither tradition describes a systemic circulation akin to modern physiology.  
- **Absences**: None of the traditions explicitly mention the heart, arteries, or a closed-loop circulatory system. The Chinese text focuses on *qi* and meridians, while the Greek and medieval European texts focus on humoral theory without detailing blood’s physical movement.

In summary, the Chinese tradition conceptualizes blood as part of a meridian-based system, while the Greek and medieval European traditions treat blood as a humor within a broader humoral framework, without explicit models of circulation.
