The corpus provides detailed discussions of acupuncture and needling exclusively within the **Chinese medical tradition**, specifically in the *Huangdi Neijing Suwen* (Huangdi Neijing). No references to acupuncture or needling appear in the **Greek** or **medieval European** sources provided. Below is a synthesis of the Chinese material:

1. **Theoretical Foundations and Principles**:  
   Acupuncture is framed as a method requiring mastery of the "classics of needling" (鍼經), involving precise timing based on natural cycles (e.g., the "coldness and warmth of the day," the "deficiency and fullness of the moon") to harmonize the body’s qi (氣) and balance physiological states [SOURCE 1]. The practice emphasizes understanding the "obscure" (冥冥) aspects of qi and pathogenic influences (虛邪, 正邪) that are not externally visible but are discernible through skilled diagnosis [SOURCE 1].

2. **Techniques and Depth of Needling**:  
   The *Suwen* specifies that acupuncture must penetrate to the correct depth for each anatomical layer (e.g., hair follicles, skin, muscles, vessels, bones) to avoid harm. For example, needling the skin without injuring the flesh prevents disturbance to the spleen, while improper needling of bones risks damaging the marrow and causing wasting diseases [SOURCE 2]. Techniques like "draining" (寫) involve precise timing during respiration (e.g., inserting the needle during maximal inhalation, withdrawing during maximal exhalation) to regulate qi [SOURCE 1].

3. **Meridian-Specific Needling**:  
   Needling is tied to specific meridians and organs, such as the "Foot Jueyin" (足厥陰) for the liver, the "Foot Shaoyang" (足少陽) for the gallbladder, and the "Pericardium" (心包胳所流) for the *Shanzhong* (膻中) [SOURCE 3]. These practices aim not only to treat disease but also to "cultivate harmony of the spirit" (修養和神) and "preserve the true essence" (全神養真之旨) [SOURCE 3].

4. **Clinical Applications**:  
   Detailed instructions are given for needling specific points to address conditions like lower back pain, with precise anatomical landmarks (e.g., "five cun above the inner ankle," "between the bone and flesh at the knee") and techniques for bloodletting (出血) [SOURCE 5]. The text also links needling to diagnostic principles, such as analyzing the "three parts nine signs" (三部九候) to detect imbalances [SOURCE 1].

**Greek and Medieval European Traditions**:  
The **Greek** sources (e.g., Hippocrates’ *On Ancient Medicine*) focus on humoral theory (heat, cold, dryness, moistness) and dietary interventions but do not mention acupuncture or needling [SOURCE 8, 9, 11]. The **medieval European** texts (e.g., Hildegard von Bingen’s *Causae et Curae*) describe bloodletting (e.g., using a "phlebotomy tool" or "brema") and herbal remedies but do not reference acupuncture or meridian-based needling [SOURCE 6, 7, 10].  

In conclusion, **acupuncture and needling are exclusively discussed in the Chinese tradition** within the *Huangdi Neijing Suwen*, with detailed technical, theoretical, and clinical elaborations. The **Greek and medieval European traditions** do not address these practices in the provided passages.
