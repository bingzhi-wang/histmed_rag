The diagnostic use of the pulse is described in the Chinese tradition with detailed attention to its qualities and associations with internal organs and pathologies, while the Greek and medieval European traditions provide less specific or absent descriptions of pulse diagnosis in the provided passages. Below is the comparative synthesis:

### **Ancient Chinese Medicine**  
The pulse is central to diagnosis, serving as a key indicator of the body’s internal state and the progression of disease. Specific pulse qualities are associated with the five zang organs: the liver pulse is likened to a string (絃), the heart to a hook (鉤), the spleen to a substitute (代), the lung to hair (毛), and the kidney to stone (石) [SOURCE 2]. Pulse characteristics such as size, smoothness, roughness, floating, and sinking are assessed by the fingers to infer the condition of the five organs [SOURCE 4]. For example, a red pulse (赤脈) is linked to heart-related pathologies, while a white pulse (白脈) suggests lung-related issues [SOURCE 4]. The pulse is also influenced by external factors like activity, emotion, and environment; for instance, night walking affects the kidney, fear impacts the liver, and startled states alter the lung’s pulse [SOURCE 3]. Additionally, the pulse’s irregularities (e.g., firm and long, soft and diffused) are correlated with specific diseases, such as a firm heart pulse indicating a curled tongue or speechlessness [SOURCE 5]. The right arm’s pulse is emphasized in some texts as particularly significant for life-threatening conditions, though this focus is less systematic than in medieval European texts [SOURCE 6].

### **Greek Medicine**  
The provided passages from Hippocrates’ *On Ancient Medicine* do not mention the pulse as a diagnostic tool. Instead, they focus on broader principles of medicine, such as the importance of understanding disease causation and the variability of medical skill [SOURCE 9]. There is no explicit discussion of pulse examination or its role in diagnosing specific conditions within the retrieved texts [SOURCE 10–11].

### **Medieval European Medicine (Hildegard von Bingen)**  
The pulse is described as a critical indicator of life and death, with particular emphasis on the right arm. The right arm’s pulse is considered especially significant because it reflects the body’s strength and the soul’s vitality; irregularities here signal impending death, while a steady, orderly pulse suggests survival [SOURCE 7]. For example, a pulse that is “orderly and temperate” in the right arm is associated with a patient’s ability to live, as it mirrors the soul’s retention of balanced breathing [SOURCE 8]. However, the texts do not elaborate on specific pulse qualities (e.g., string-like, hook-like) or their associations with organs, as seen in Chinese medicine. The focus is more on the pulse’s general rhythm and its connection to the soul’s state rather than detailed pathological correlations.

### **Summary of Differences**  
- **Chinese Medicine**: The pulse is a detailed diagnostic tool, with specific qualities (e.g., 絃, 鉤) linked to organs and pathologies, and its assessment is integrated with observations of color, emotion, and environmental factors [SOURCE 2–6].  
- **Greek Medicine**: The retrieved passages do not discuss the pulse as a diagnostic method, focusing instead on theoretical principles of medicine [SOURCE 9–11].  
- **Medieval European Medicine**: The pulse is viewed as a vital sign tied to the soul’s state, with the right arm’s pulse emphasized for life-threatening conditions, but without the systematic categorization of pulse qualities seen in Chinese texts [SOURCE 7–8].  

The Greek tradition lacks explicit references to pulse diagnosis in the provided sources, while the Chinese and medieval European traditions both associate the pulse with life and death but differ in their methodological and conceptual frameworks.
