Hildegard of Bingen’s *Causae et Curae* associates **melancholy** (melancolia) and **black bile** (fel nigrum or livor) as interconnected humoral imbalances that contribute to physical and mental disorders. The text describes a causal chain where unresolved anger generates **bile**, which, if unchecked, transforms into **melancholy**, producing a "black cloud" that affects the brain and body. Specifically:

1. **Anger and Bile**: When a person silently endures anger, bile ("calidum fumum") is initially produced but may cease. However, if anger persists, it "excites" melancholy, which emits a "nigerrimam nebulam" (black cloud) that interacts with bile, leading to madness and physical symptoms like trembling veins and abdominal distress ([SOURCE 1]; [SOURCE 2]).

2. **Melancholy as Black Bile**: Melancholy is explicitly linked to **black bile**, described as "nigra" (black) and "amarissimum fumum" (bitter smoke). This black bile is portrayed as harmful, causing weakness, madness, and systemic decay when it "contracts" the "infirmitatem debilium humorum" ([SOURCE 6]; [SOURCE 7]).

3. **Diet and Humoral Balance**: The text notes that **bile** increases with "bonis et suavis cibis" (good and sweet foods) and decreases with "malis" (bad foods), while **melancholy** behaves inversely—decreasing with good foods and increasing with "amari et immundis" (bitter and unclean foods). This suggests a dietary influence on the balance between bile and melancholy ([SOURCE 4]).

4. **Pathological Effects**: Melancholy is depicted as a corrupting force that "exhales all evil," causing "weakness to the brain and to the heart," "sadness," and "doubt of all consolation" ([SOURCE 6]). Black bile is similarly tied to decay, with its "nocivus livor" (harmful black bile) described as contracting weakness and requiring expulsion through purging ([SOURCE 7]).

The passages do not explicitly compare melancholy and black bile to concepts in Greek or Chinese medicine, nor do they reference diagnostic or therapeutic methods beyond humoral theory. The focus is strictly on the **interplay between anger, bile, and melancholy** as a pathogenic process.
