In Hippocrates' *Prognostic*, bodily signs used to forecast disease outcomes include **sputum characteristics**, **urine analysis**, **respiratory function**, **pain patterns**, **thirst**, **body temperature distribution**, and **response to treatments**. These signs are evaluated in relation to seasonal and epidemiological contexts [SOURCE 1].  

Specifically, **favorable symptoms** (indicating potential recovery) include:  
- Easy tolerance of the disease, free respiration, absence of pain, and ease in expectorating sputum [SOURCE 2].  
- Uniform body warmth, absence of thirst, and favorable urine, feces, sleep, and sweating [SOURCE 2].  

**Unfavorable symptoms** (indicating mortality) include:  
- Persistence of pain despite treatments (venesection, purging, or regimen), suggesting suppuration [SOURCE 2].  
- Dense, labored respiration; difficulty expectorating sputum; intense thirst; uneven body temperature (e.g., hot abdomen/sides and cold extremities); and unfavorable urine, feces, sleep, and sweating [SOURCE 2].  
- Sputum containing pus and bile together, particularly if empyema develops on the seventh day of illness, which is deemed highly fatal [SOURCE 2].  

The text also emphasizes that **symptoms must be interpreted in context**, such as the season, epidemic conditions, and regional patterns observed in places like Libya, Delos, and Scythia [SOURCE 1]. For example, a patient with expectorated pus and bile who fails to improve by the fourteenth day is predicted to die unless "favorable symptoms supervene" [SOURCE 2].  

No other bodily signs (e.g., pulse, tongue, or external injuries) are explicitly mentioned in the provided passages for this work. The analysis is grounded in **internal symptoms**, **fluids (sputum, urine)**, and **physiological responses** rather than anatomical or external indicators.
